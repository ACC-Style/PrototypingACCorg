## Zone: The problem — burnout in cardiology

**H2**  
Why Well-Being Matters

**Intro (draft)**  
Burnout is not a personal failing. It is a occupational syndrome with measurable consequences for cardiologists, their families, and the patients they serve.

### What is burnout? *(Toolkit)*

Adapted from the World Health Organization, burnout has three dimensions:

| Dimension | What it means |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Exhaustion** | Feeling overextended by work demands and depleted of the emotional and physical resources you need to recover |
| **Cynicism** | A negative, callous, or detached response to the job — loss of idealism and dehumanization of others |
| **Inefficacy** | Feeling incompetent and unproductive — a lack of achievement at work |

### How common is it? *(Toolkit)*

2018 Medscape Physician Lifestyle Report data cited in the ACC Toolkit:

- **40%** of cardiologists experienced burnout
- **12%** felt depressed; **3%** had clinical depression
- Cardiology ranked among specialties with **higher burnout** and **lower happiness**
- Only **17%** of cardiologists would consider seeking professional health support — the **lowest rate among all medical specialties**

**Consequences extend beyond the individual** *(Toolkit)*

| Area | Impact |
|------|--------|
| Coping | Increased alcohol use, unhealthy eating, self-isolation |
| Suicide risk | Roughly **double** the general population; **130% higher** among women physicians and **40% higher** among men compared to the general population |

## Zone: Risk factors — why cardiology is hard

**H2**  
What Drives Burnout in Cardiology

**Intro (draft)**  
Burnout rarely has a single cause. These risk factors — drawn from ACC's well-being work — often compound over a career.

*(Toolkit)*

| Category | Risk factors |
|----------|--------------|
| **Practice demands** | Administrative burden; long training paths, especially subspecialization; supply-demand mismatch (projected shortage of 4,000+ cardiologists); more patients and longer hours |
| **Life stage pressures** | Raising a family; student loan debt; new mortgages; retirement planning |
| **Academic tension** | More clinical duty, less time for research, teaching, and scholarly work |

**Body (draft)**  
Individual resilience matters — but these are system problems. That is why ACC pairs personal tools with workplace interventions and national policy.

## 2A. Care for Yourself

**H2**  
Care for Yourself

**Intro (draft)**  
Stress keeps you going. Burnout depletes you. Know the difference — and act before symptoms become a crisis.

---

### Build a self-care plan *(Toolkit)*

**H3**  
Daily Practices That Prevent Burnout

**Intro (draft)**  
Self-care is not indulgence — it is maintenance. Elements of a self-care plan include journaling, meditation, yoga, reading, music, mindfulness, stretching, tai chi, breath techniques, and regular exercise.

| Domain | Practices |
|--------|-----------|
| **Sleep hygiene** | Protect rest as a clinical skill, not a luxury |
| **Mindfulness & meditation** | Practice being fully aware and present; build self-awareness and reduce stress |
| **Movement** | Yoga, regular exercise, relaxation techniques |
| **Connection** | Stay connected with friends, family, and community |

**Detect early and act** *(Toolkit)*

- Watch for deteriorating health before symptoms become severe
- Make a personal vision plan and prioritize goals
- Set work-life boundaries and hold them
- Schedule time for self-care, hobbies, and the people who matter

### Take action under pressure

*(Evergreen — adapted from semi-retired COVID pages.)*

**H3**  
Self-Care During High-Stress Periods

To care for others, care for yourself first. Practices adapted from the Center for the Study of Traumatic Stress:

| Practice | Action |
|----------|--------|
| **Remember self-care** | Monitor stress; take breaks; exercise; get outside |
| **Stay connected** | Check in with colleagues, family, and supervisors |
| **Respect differences** | In yourself, colleagues, and patients — some need to talk, others need space |
| **Limit media exposure** | Alarming content raises stress and lowers effectiveness |
| **Self check-ins** | Watch for prolonged sadness, sleep problems, intrusive memories, hopelessness |
| **Honor your service** | Recognize the work you and your colleagues do |
| **Practice compassion** | Patience and gratitude sustain the team |

**Source:** [Center for the Study of Traumatic Stress, Uniformed Services University](https://www.cstsonline.org/assets/media/documents/CSTS_FS_Sustaining_Well_Being_Healthcare_Personnel_during.pdf)

---

**H3**  
Manage Stress and Anxiety

Psychosocial well-being matters as much as physical health. Feeling stressed under pressure is normal — it is not proof you cannot do your job.

**Steady yourself — ask:**

- What happens to your body when worries mount?
- How worried are you? What do you fear most?
- What usually helps you handle worries?

**Techniques**

| Technique | How |
|-----------|-----|
| **Diaphragmatic breathing** | Inhale and expand your belly; pause; exhale slowly to a count of five. Repeat four times. |
| **Box breathing** | Slow, deep breaths to heighten focus and relieve stress |
| **Guided imagery** | Envision a calming place; engage all senses |
| **Mindfulness** | Especially effective for time pressure and excessive workload |

---

**H3**  
Resilience Reboot — 30-Minute Check-In

*(From Resilience Building — semi-retired.)*

Resilience only works when you pause to recharge. Set aside 30 minutes alone or with a trusted colleague.

**Six domains:** Vision · Composure · Reasoning · Tenacity · Collaboration · Health

**Nine questions**

1. What matters to me most right now?
2. What is my greatest strength at this time?
3. How can I channel that strength toward what matters most?
4. What is one step I need to take right now?
5. What can I safely stop doing for now?
6. Who is one person I can involve?
7. What is the most important reason I will not neglect my vision, myself, and others?
8. I will take care of my physical health by: ___
9. I will take care of my emotional health by: ___

## 2B. Strengthen Your Team

**H2**  
Strengthen Your Team

**Intro (draft)**  
Telling burned-out clinicians to "be more resilient" is not enough. Sustainable well-being requires changes in how practices run, how cultures support people, and how institutions invest in their teams. ACC's model identifies three reciprocal domains — each reinforces the others.

*(Toolkit — based on Bohman et al., NEJM Catalyst, 2017)*

---

### The three domains of workplace well-being

**H3**  
Practice Efficiency · Culture of Wellness · Personal Resilience

| Domain | What it means | Why it matters |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| **Workplace efficiency** | Reduce friction in daily practice | Administrative burden is a top burnout driver in cardiology |
| **Culture of wellness** | Build institutional commitment to clinician health | Environment shapes whether people seek help or hide struggle |
| **Personal resilience** | Coaching, peer support, and life planning | Individual tools work better when the system supports their use |

**Body (draft)**  
These domains are reciprocal. A wellness program fails if clinicians drown in paperwork. Efficiency gains erode in a culture that rewards overwork. Personal resilience training alone cannot fix a broken system.

[Download the full ACC Clinician Well-Being Toolkit (PPT)](https://www.acc.org/~/media/Non-Clinical/Files-PDFs-Excel-MS-Word-etc/2020/05/26/Clinician-Well-Being-Toolkit.pptx) to facilitate local discussions.

### Workplace efficiency — reduce the burden

**H3**  
Make Practice Sustainable

*(Toolkit)*

| Intervention | Action |
|--------------|--------|
| **Reduce administrative burden** | Audit non-clinical tasks; remove what does not serve patients |
| **Medical scribes** | Hire and train scribes to offload documentation |
| **Protected admin time** | Block dedicated time in clinician schedules for charts and inbox |
| **Team-based care** | Designated health care teams, daily huddles, shared workflows |
| **Clinician-led design** | Involve clinicians in developing clinic flows and processes |

**Body (draft)**  
Cardiologists cite administrative burden among the strongest burnout drivers. These are structural fixes — not self-help — and they require leadership buy-in.

### Personal resilience at the organizational level

**H3**  
Support the Whole Clinician

*(Toolkit — organizational interventions, not individual tips)*

| Intervention | Action |
|--------------|--------|
| **Coaching** | Stress management, mindfulness, and wellness techniques |
| **Peer dialogue** | Small groups to share stories of managing burnout |
| **Financial wellness** | Financial counselors for early-career professionals; resources for retirement, mortgages, and family planning |
| **Employee Assistance Programs (EAP)** | Connect staff to licensed counselors and resources for childcare, eldercare, legal, and financial needs |

**What is an EAP?** *(Toolkit)*

A work-based program staffed by licensed professionals — clinical social workers, mental health counselors, and substance abuse specialists. Services may include counseling (phone or in-person), well-being coaching, and referrals. Sessions are typically limited in number and protected under HIPAA. **Contact your Human Resources office** to learn what your institution offers.

### Lead and protect your team

**H3**  
Communicating With Your Team in High-Stress Times

*(Adapted from Crisis Communication — semi-retired.)*

Communication builds trust — or breaks it. When people are stressed, they struggle to hear, understand, and remember information. Be concise, consistent, and clear.

**Four trust pillars**

1. **Caring and empathy** — Acknowledge worry; let others voice concern  
2. **Dedication and commitment** — Address and confirm team needs  
3. **Competence and expertise** — Share knowledge; cite trusted sources  
4. **Honesty and openness** — Be transparent about facts and feelings  

**Before you communicate**

1. Anticipate your audience's needs and fears  
2. Prepare around those needs  
3. Practice before you deliver  
4. Solicit input and concerns  

**Key principles**

- Lead with empathy; limit messages to three to five
- Repeat critical information at the beginning and end (primacy/recency)
- Use graphics, analogies, and narratives to aid recall
- Cite sources; reiterate key topics; invite ongoing dialogue
